1.- Background

Setting the scene

Before the XXIst century, the heart was generally regarded as an organ lacking a stem cell population in adults. By the 2000s the existence of a putative cardiac stem cell population had already become subject of intense investigation and the first reports discussing the potential of adult stem cells in cardiac regeneration appeared on the literature. Initially, publications reported cardiac progenitor stem cells identified by the expression of the markers c-kit and Sca1, with seemingly regenerative properties in infarcted myocardium (Beltrami et al., 2003; Oh et al., 2003; Orlic et al., 2001).

Since then, at least a dozen different markers have been proposed (Noseda et al., 2015a), however, there is still no consensus on their role in cardiac regeneration and the mechanism through which it is accomplished, with some authors even still challenging the notion of cardiac stem cells altogether (Kretzschmar et al., 2018).

Cardiac Progenitor Stem Cells

Dr. Michela’s group studies the cardiac stromal compartment including the Cardiac Progenitor/stem Cells (CPC), working with both human and mouse samples. Figure 1 summarizes the major known cardiac cell types, with CPCs on the top left corner.

Using a murine model, a previous characterisation of cells in the hematopoietic negative lineage fraction of the heart via flow cytometry and single cell qRT-PCR, revealed a total of four subpopulations with partial overlapping (see Figure 2) characterised by the following markers (Noseda et al., 2015b):

  • Stem cell antigen-1 (Sca1): Canonical stem cell marker, it has been associated with myocardial renewal in adult hearts (Uchida et al., 2013, p. 1).
  • Side population phenotype (SP): Found in both developing and adult hearts, SP is thought to be a surrogate marker for multipotency (Challen and Little, 2006; Goodell et al., 1996).
  • Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGFRα): Associated with cell growth and differentiation, PDGFRα+ve cells show the most cardiogenic and clonogenic properties in Noseda et al., 2015b.
  • PECAM-1/CD31: PECAM-1, also known as CD31, is an endothelial marker that was found linked with endothelial fates in the cardiac progenitor stem cell population (as opposed to PDGFRα).

Figure 1: Diagram illustrating the different types of known cell types in an adult heart and relative unknown aspects. Note that we are interested in the Cardiac Progenitor stem Cell population (CPC), especially those with high cardiogenic potential and thus PDGFRα+ve. Modified from Massaia et al., 2018.

As will be shown on the following section, this project revolves around the PDGFRα+ve population.

Figure 2: Schematic representation of the four cardiac subpopulations defined by Sca1, PDGFRα, CD31 and SP. SP enriches for the clonogenic fraction and PDGFRα precisely tracks the cardiogenic molecular signature. Data from this project focuses on the PDGFRα+ve population. SP: Side population phenotype, CD31: Platelet endothelial cell adhesion molecule, Sca1: Stem cell antigen-1, PDGFRα: Platelet-derived growth factor. Based on Noseda et al., 2015b.
